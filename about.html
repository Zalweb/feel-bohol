History

The Sandugo — A Blood Compact for the Ages

On March 25, 1565, on the shores of what is now Tagbilaran City, a ritual took place that would be remembered for centuries. Datu Sikatuna, chieftain of Bohol, and Miguel López de Legazpi, commander of the Spanish fleet, each drew a few drops of blood, mixed it with wine, and drank — sealing a treaty of friendship known as the Sandugo. It stands as the first international treaty of friendship in Philippine history.


Every July, Tagbilaran City erupts into a month-long celebration in honour of this historic pact. The Sandugo Festival features spectacular street dancing competitions, cultural reenactments, the Miss Bohol Sandugo Beauty Pageant, and traditional arts showcases that draw visitors from across the world.

Heritage

Francisco Dagohoy — The Longest Rebellion

Francisco Dagohoy launched a rebellion in 1744 that would last an extraordinary 85 years — the longest in Philippine history. Sparked by the refusal of a Jesuit priest to grant a Christian burial to his brother, Dagohoy rallied thousands of Boholanos against Spanish colonial rule, protesting forced labour and excessive taxes.


His community of approximately 20,000 followers established an independent enclave deep in the mountains of Bohol, evading Spanish military campaigns for generations. The rebellion outlasted multiple governor-generals and only ended in 1829 — a testament to the indomitable spirit of the Boholano people.

Architecture

Baclayon Church — Built in Coral and Egg White

Founded in 1596 by Jesuit priests, the Immaculate Conception Parish Church of Baclayon is one of the oldest stone churches in the Philippines. Its coral stone walls were completed in 1727 — bound together not with ordinary mortar but with egg whites, giving the structure a peculiar resilience that has survived centuries of weather and earthquakes.


Declared a National Cultural Treasure and a National Historical Landmark, Baclayon Church is home to a museum filled with religious artifacts, antique vestments, and rare manuscripts. Although severely damaged by a 7.2-magnitude earthquake in 2013, the church was painstakingly restored by 2018 and stands once again as a beacon of Bohol's colonial heritage.
